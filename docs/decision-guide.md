# Decision guide

GateSignal is most useful when the group agrees on the decision question before debating the score.

## Before the review

- Name one accountable decision owner.
- State the next commitment precisely: amount, scope, reversibility, and learning objective.
- Adapt the criteria and hard gates to the actual concept.
- Record the source and limitation of important evidence.
- Separate scenario assumptions instead of negotiating one “comfortable” forecast.
- Give an independent reviewer access to the strongest counter-case.

## During the review

Read the evidence in this order:

1. hard-gate failures;
2. weakest evidence on the highest-weight claims;
3. downside, reference, and upside economics;
4. forecast-assumption bridge;
5. untreated high and critical risks;
6. disconfirming evidence and outside-view comparison; and
7. the next bounded learning milestone.

The decimal score should never become the agenda. If reviewers disagree, record whether the disagreement concerns the criterion, weight, score, evidence, threshold, forecast, or consequence. Those are different problems.

## Interpreting “Consider go”

“Consider go” means no built-in stop or hold rule currently fires. It does not mean the project is good, safe, compliant, affordable, or strategically superior. Before approving any commitment, the decision owner should record:

- the actual disposition;
- the rationale and key dissent;
- the maximum funding or resource commitment;
- the evidence the next step must create;
- stop or redesign conditions; and
- the next review date.

## Learning-oriented commitments

When uncertainty is large, prefer a reversible next step that attacks the most consequential unknown. A good milestone says what will be observed, how the observation changes the decision, who owns it, the spending cap, and when the review occurs. “Learn more” is not a milestone.

## Avoiding common traps

- Do not improve the score merely because substantial money has already been spent.
- Do not treat scenario probability as measured confidence unless it actually comes from a defensible statistical process.
- Do not offset a failed hard gate with commercial attractiveness.
- Do not call a mitigation complete without an observable trigger and an executable response.
- Do not accept one project in isolation if it displaces a better portfolio use of scarce people or capital.
